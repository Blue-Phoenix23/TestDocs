• Summary
  • 1925
  • Highly publicized legal case pitting hs teacher John Scopes against Tennessee’s Butler Act, which banned teaching of evolution in high school
  • Scopes taught evolution in his high school
  • William Jennings Bryan and Clarence Darrow were attorneys and accomplished orators, had opposing views from each other
  • Challenge constitutionality of the bill, advocate for the theory of evolution, and promote American Civil Liberties Union (ACLU)
  • ACLU wanted Scopes to admit to teaching evolution, even though he taught physics and math, not biology. While he wasn’t sure if he taught evolution specifically, his materials did include it.
  • After the trial, Scopes was found guilty
  • Supreme Court appeal led to upheld of law and acquittal of Scopes for an unreasonable fine
  • Effects - Butler Act was upheld, anti-evolution movement continued
  • Other states began passing similar acts
  • World became more aware of evolutionary theory and evidences
  • Charles Darwin’s The Origin of the Species and The Descendant of Man
  • Fundamentalist Movement
  • Believed in strict interpretations of the Bible, which contradicted theory of evolution
  • Anti-evolution pressures in Tennessee led to House of Representatives passing Butler Act, an unenforced law preventing evolution education
  • ACLU immediately challenged the passing of the act
  • William Jennings Bryan - prosecutor and ex-presidential nominee known for anti-evolution beliefs
  • Wanted to defend the Butler Act and denounce evolution
  • Modernist Movement
  • Supported liberal biblical interpretations and a coexistence of science and religion
  • Clarence Darrow - famous attorney, acted for the defense in the Leopold and Loeb murder trial, leading member of the ACLU
  • Proceedings
  • Although Darrow cited that the law inhibited freedom of religion, Judge John Raulston disregarded the argument, saying the focus was on Scope’s actions rather than the law’s constitutionality
  • Darrow also used Bryan as a witness, showing how literal Biblical interpretations were nonsensical in current day situations
